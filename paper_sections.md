## 3. Generative Model

We formalise counterfactual temporal framing as policy selection within a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP), following the active inference framework (Parr, Pezzulo & Friston, 2022). The agent maintains beliefs over a factored hidden state space and selects among temporally oriented policies that differentially redistribute precision across state transitions.

### 3.1 Factored State Space

The hidden state $s = (v, e, f)$ is factored into three dimensions:

- **Valence** $v \in \{0, \ldots, K{-}1\}$: The agent's affective state, discretised at granularity $K$. This is the key parameter corresponding to the *granularity of the higher-order state space* described in the abstract. At $K{=}2$, the agent has only "very negative" and "very positive" as attractor basins; at $K{=}8$, intermediate states (e.g., irritation, mild contentment) become representable.

- **Energy** $e \in \{0, \ldots, M{-}1\}$: An interoceptive resource estimate, modelling the metabolic or energetic budget available for action. This hidden state enables the switching dynamical system structure underlying bipolar oscillation: manic episodes deplete the energy budget; when prediction error about resource availability crosses a threshold, the system transitions into a conservation regime (depressive phase).

- **Temporal frame** $f \in \{\text{PAST}, \text{PRESENT}, \text{FUTURE}\}$: The currently active counterfactual orientation. PAST corresponds to backward-facing precision modulation (autobiographical counterfactual simulation); PRESENT to standard perception–action coupling; FUTURE to forward-facing precision modulation (prospective counterfactual simulation toward a "solution state").

The total state space has dimensionality $K \times M \times 3$. Under the factored representation, transition and observation matrices decompose into factor-specific components, yielding a compact specification.

### 3.2 Observation Model

The agent receives three observation modalities, each generated by a likelihood mapping $\mathbf{A}_m$:

1. **Environmental feedback** $o_{\text{ext}} \in \{\text{negative}, \text{neutral}, \text{positive}\}$: Depends on both valence and energy. Higher valence and energy increase the probability of positive feedback, modelling the bidirectional relationship between affective state and environmental engagement.

2. **Interoceptive signal** $o_{\text{int}} \in \{\text{depleted}, \text{neutral}, \text{energised}\}$: Depends on the true energy state, with precision controlled by the parameter $\omega_e$. When $\omega_e$ is high, the agent accurately reads its metabolic state; when low, the interoceptive signal is blurred, and the agent cannot distinguish depletion from energy availability.

3. **Felt valence** $o_{\text{val}} \in \{0, \ldots, K{-}1\}$: A near-identity mapping from the valence state, providing the agent with (noisy) access to its own affective level.

Critically, the *agent's generative model* of $\mathbf{A}_{\text{int}}$ may differ from the environment's true observation-generating process. This mismatch — the agent's interoceptive model being less precise than reality — is the computational mechanism underlying mania: the environment provides accurate energy depletion signals, but the agent's blurred $\mathbf{A}_{\text{int}}$ prevents these signals from updating beliefs about resource availability.

### 3.3 Transition Model: Temporal Framing as Policy

Each action (temporal framing mode) defines a distinct transition structure $\mathbf{B}_a = \mathbf{B}_v^{(a)} \otimes \mathbf{B}_e^{(a)} \otimes \mathbf{B}_f^{(a)}$, where $\otimes$ denotes the Kronecker product over factored transition matrices.

**RECALL (past-oriented framing).**
The valence transition $\mathbf{B}_v^{\text{RECALL}}$ pulls the affective state toward the prior $\mathbf{D}$ — specifically, toward positively valenced states. The strength of this pull is controlled by $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ through a sigmoidal gating function $\alpha = \sigma(\pi_{\text{pos}} - \theta)$. When $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ is high, RECALL effectively generates positive counterfactuals grounded in a confident self-model (meaning, self-fulfilment, optimism); when $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ is low, the pull is negligible, and past-oriented counterfactuals fail to sustain positive affect. Energy cost is minimal.

**ENGAGE (present-oriented framing).**
Standard perception–action coupling. Valence evolves as a random walk centred on the current state, driven primarily by environmental feedback. Energy depletes at a moderate rate.

**FUTURATE (future-oriented framing).**
The valence transition pulls toward the most positive state — the "solution state" — modelling the generation of a highly precise prospective counterfactual. Energy cost is high, reflecting the intensive environmental sampling and goal-directed behaviour associated with future-oriented framing. The frame transition is strongly absorbing: once in FUTURE mode, the agent tends to remain there.

**REST (conservation).**
Valence drifts toward neutral, energy recovers, and the frame opens to any orientation. This models the conservation prior that activates when resource depletion is detected.

### 3.4 Preferences and Prior

The preference vectors $\mathbf{C}_m$ encode the agent's prior expectations about desirable observations: positive environmental feedback, feeling energised, and positive valence. These correspond to the organism's homeostatic setpoints.

The prior $\mathbf{D}$ over initial states is shaped by $\pi_{\text{pos}}$: higher $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ concentrates mass on positive valence states, reflecting a confident, positively biased self-model. This prior serves a dual role — it initialises beliefs *and* provides the attractor for RECALL transitions.

### 3.5 Clinical Parameters

Three parameters capture the computational dimensions of vulnerability described in the abstract:

| Parameter | Role | Low value | High value |
|-----------|------|-----------|------------|
| $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ | Precision on positive self-beliefs | Cannot generate positive past counterfactuals | Stable autobiographical narrative sustains affect |
| $K$ | Affective granularity | Coarse attractor landscape; catastrophic transitions | Fine-grained affect; smooth regulation |
| $\omega_e$ | Interoceptive precision (energy) | Cannot detect resource depletion (mania risk) | Accurate metabolic self-monitoring |


## 4. Affective Readout

Following Pattisapu, Verbelen, Pitliya, Kiefer & Albarracin (2024), we derive both valence and arousal from the agent's free energy, grounding the simulation in a circumplex model of emotion.

### 4.1 Valence

Valence is computed as a reward prediction error — the difference between the utility of the actual observation and its expected value under the agent's predictive model:

$$V = U - \mathbb{E}[U]$$

where $U = \sum_m \log P(o_m | C_m)$ is the utility of the observed outcome given preferences, and $\mathbb{E}[U] = \sum_m \sum_{o} Q(o_m | \pi) \log P(o_m | C_m)$ is the expected utility under predicted observations $Q(o_m|\pi) = \mathbf{A}_m \, q_{\text{pred}}$. Positive valence signals a "better than expected" outcome; negative valence signals worse than expected.

### 4.2 Arousal

Arousal is the entropy of the posterior belief distribution:

$$A = H[Q(s|o)] = -\sum_s Q(s|o) \log Q(s|o)$$

normalised by maximum entropy $\log |\mathcal{S}|$ to yield $A_{\text{norm}} \in [0, 1]$. High arousal reflects posterior uncertainty — the agent does not know what state it is in — and is functionally linked to the amygdala's role as a learning signal for allostatic regulation (Feldman-Barrett, 2017).

### 4.3 Circumplex Transformation

To map the agent's trajectory onto the circumplex model, we centre arousal as $A_c = 2 A_{\text{norm}} - 1 \in [-1, 1]$, so that low posterior entropy (certainty) corresponds to calm ($A_c < 0$) and high entropy (uncertainty) corresponds to alertness ($A_c > 0$). The polar transformation $r = \sqrt{V^2 + A_c^2}$, $\theta = \arctan(A_c / V)$ then places the agent's emotional state within the standard circumplex (Russell, 1980), with 0° = Happy, 90° = Alert, 180° = Sad, 270° = Calm.

### 4.4 Temporal Derivatives (Joffily & Coricelli)

In parallel, we track the variational free energy $F(t)$ at each timestep and compute:
- **Valence (J-C)**: $\tanh(-\Delta F / \tau)$, capturing the *rate* of free energy change
- **Anticipation**: $-\Delta^2 F$, capturing curvature — positive for hope, negative for fear

This dual readout allows us to examine both the *momentary* affective state (Pattisapu: how does the current observation compare to expectation?) and the *trajectory* of affective dynamics (Joffily-Coricelli: is free energy increasing or decreasing over time?).


## 5. Simulation Design

### 5.1 Clinical Profiles

We define three parameter configurations corresponding to the phenotypes described in the abstract:

**Healthy.** $\pi_{\text{pos}} = 5.0$, $K = 8$, $\omega_e = 5.0$. High-precision positive self-beliefs enable effective RECALL. Fine-grained affective state space permits smooth regulation. Accurate interoception supports adaptive energy management.

**Depressive.** $\pi_{\text{pos}} = 0.5$, $K = 2$, $\omega_e = 5.0$. Low precision on positive beliefs renders RECALL ineffective — past-oriented counterfactuals cannot sustain positive affect. Binary affective granularity ($K{=}2$) means the agent can only occupy "very negative" or "very positive" states, with no intermediate regulation. Interoception remains accurate; the agent *can* detect its state but *cannot* change it through narrative.

**Manic.** $\pi_{\text{pos}} = 4.0$, $K = 2$, $\omega_e = 0.5$. Moderate positive beliefs, but critically, low interoceptive precision. The agent cannot accurately read its energy depletion, leading to sustained FUTURATE selection even as true energy depletes. The binary affective state space ($K{=}2$) ensures that when the crash comes, it is catastrophic — a direct jump from the positive to the negative basin.

### 5.2 Experimental Protocol

Each profile is run for $T = 300$ timesteps in a stochastic environment with volatility $p = 0.3$ (probability of random valence perturbation per step). The environment provides well-calibrated observations; pathology arises from the agent's model being miscalibrated, not from the environment being hostile. The same random seed ensures identical environmental conditions across profiles.

### 5.3 Granularity Sweep

To isolate the effect of affective granularity, we fix $\pi_{\text{pos}} = 3.0$ and $\omega_e = 0.8$ (a "bipolar-vulnerable" configuration) and sweep $K \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ across 5 independent runs per value. We measure: (a) valence variance (oscillation amplitude), (b) jump-size distribution $|\Delta v_t|$, (c) action selection proportions.

### 5.4 Parameter Landscape

We sweep $\pi_{\text{pos}} \in [0.1, 8.0]$ and $\omega_e \in [0.1, 8.0]$ over a $10 \times 10$ grid (fixing $K{=}4$, $T{=}200$) and compute mean valence, valence variance, mean true energy, and FUTURATE selection fraction. This generates a two-dimensional clinical landscape in which the three profiles occupy distinct regions.


## 6. Results

### 6.1 Phenotype Differentiation

The three clinical profiles produce qualitatively distinct trajectories (Figure 1).

The **healthy** agent maintains high mean valence ($\bar{v} = 0.66$) and near-maximal energy ($\bar{e} = 0.97$). Policy selection is dominated by ENGAGE, with periodic RECALL episodes that sustain positive affect. The Joffily-Coricelli valence signal oscillates symmetrically around zero, indicating balanced prediction error dynamics.

The **depressive** agent exhibits mean valence near floor ($\bar{v} = 0.05$) and rapid energy depletion ($\bar{e} = 0.06$). Crucially, the agent selects FUTURATE at high rates despite its ineffectiveness — it attempts future-oriented framing but, with $K{=}2$, can only generate undifferentiated positive counterfactuals that do not resolve the uncertainty driving its distress. RECALL is equally ineffective due to low $\pi_{\text{pos}}$. The result is the pattern described in the abstract: "uncertainty remains diffusely distributed across temporal horizons, as it is neither attenuated through positively biased autobiographical counterfactuals nor reduced via optimistic prospective counterfactuals."

The **manic** agent shows a biphasic trajectory. For the first ~50 timesteps, valence is high and FUTURATE dominates — this is the manic phase, characterised by "excessively expansive and dysregulated goal-directed behaviour." Energy depletes steadily, but the agent's blurred interoceptive model ($\omega_e = 0.5$) prevents it from detecting the depletion. When true energy reaches critical levels, environmental feedback becomes consistently negative (depleted agents receive worse outcomes), overwhelming even the blurred interoceptive beliefs. The agent crashes into the negative valence basin and transitions to REST. This crash is abrupt due to $K{=}2$: there are no intermediate affective states to buffer the transition.

### 6.2 Granularity and Oscillation Dynamics

The granularity sweep (Figure 3) provides direct computational evidence for the abstract's claim that "reduced granularity in higher-order belief distributions constrains the generation of future-oriented counterfactuals for resolving uncertainty."

Valence variance decreases monotonically with granularity: 0.15 at $K{=}2$, 0.065 at $K{=}4$, 0.041 at $K{=}6$, and 0.030 at $K{=}8$ — a five-fold reduction. The jump-size distribution is bimodal at $K{=}2$, with substantial mass at $|\Delta v| = 1.0$ (full-scale catastrophic transitions), whereas for $K \geq 4$ it concentrates at small values. In active inference terms, this corresponds to the difference between a transition matrix $\mathbf{B}_v$ that can only shift between two attractor basins (catastrophic) and one that supports gradual belief updating across a smooth landscape.

This result formalises the clinical observation that emotional granularity — the ability to make fine-grained distinctions among affective states — is a protective factor against mood instability (Barrett, 2017). In our model, the protection is mechanistic: higher $K$ increases the resolution of the $\mathbf{B}_v$ matrix, enabling the agent to make smaller steps through the affective landscape rather than being forced into all-or-nothing transitions.

### 6.3 Circumplex Emotional Trajectories

Mapping valence and arousal onto the circumplex (Figure 6) reveals distinct emotional signatures for each phenotype.

The **healthy** agent's trajectory occupies the Excited–Alert quadrant (positive valence, moderate arousal), reflecting an engaged agent whose outcomes regularly exceed expectations while maintaining moderate posterior uncertainty — consistent with adaptive exploration.

The **depressive** agent drifts from Alert/Angry (high arousal, negative valence) toward the Depressed/Sad region (low arousal, negative valence) over time. This trajectory matches the clinical phenomenology: initial agitated distress gives way to resignation as the agent accumulates evidence that its counterfactual strategies (both RECALL and FUTURATE) are ineffective.

The **manic** agent occupies a narrow band at the Angry–Excited boundary (high arousal, fluctuating valence). The tight clustering reflects the agent's inability to move beyond its $K{=}2$ affective resolution even as its metabolic state deteriorates. This is consistent with the clinical picture of mania as a high-energy, high-arousal state with labile valence — the agent oscillates between "better than expected" and "worse than expected" without the affective granularity to stabilise.

### 6.4 Parameter Landscape

The parameter sweep (Figure 4) reveals a clinical landscape with three identifiable regions:

- **Low $\omega_e$, any $\pi_{\text{pos}}$**: Consistently low mean valence and depleted energy. This is the "manic crash" zone — regardless of self-belief precision, poor interoception leads to unsustainable FUTURATE selection and energy depletion.

- **Low $\pi_{\text{pos}}$, high $\omega_e$**: Moderate-low valence despite accurate interoception. This is the depressive zone — the agent can track its state but cannot generate the positive counterfactuals needed to sustain affect.

- **High $\pi_{\text{pos}}$, high $\omega_e$**: Highest mean valence and stable energy. The healthy zone — both narrative resources (effective RECALL) and self-monitoring (accurate interoception) are available.


## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Narrative Regulation as Policy Selection

The model formalises the paper's central claim — that "strategic narratives are dynamically self-maintaining structures" — by casting temporal framing as active inference policy selection. RECALL, ENGAGE, FUTURATE, and REST are not merely cognitive strategies; they are policies with distinct transition dynamics that the agent selects by minimising expected free energy. A narrative "suffers" when the policy it implements generates persistent prediction error (rising VFE), and it "defends itself" when the EFE landscape favours its continuation.

The depressive phenotype illustrates narrative failure: RECALL cannot pull valence toward positive states (low $\pi_{\text{pos}}$ deflates the D-prior attractor), and FUTURATE cannot resolve uncertainty (low $K$ means the "solution state" is too coarse to guide action). The manic phenotype illustrates narrative disconnection from evidence: FUTURATE successfully pulls valence upward *in the model* but at an energetic cost invisible to the agent's blurred interoceptive model.

### 7.2 Granularity as a Transdiagnostic Vulnerability

The granularity parameter $K$ captures a dimension of vulnerability that is orthogonal to both positive-belief precision and interoceptive accuracy. Low $K$ is not a mood state but a structural property of the generative model — it constrains the *resolution* at which affect can be represented, regardless of the agent's current valence.

This has direct implications for intervention. Increasing emotional granularity (through psychoeducation, mindfulness, or affect labelling) does not change the agent's beliefs about itself ($\pi_{\text{pos}}$) or its ability to monitor energy ($\omega_e$); instead, it changes the *dimensionality of the space* in which affective regulation occurs. Our simulation shows that this alone reduces oscillation amplitude five-fold — a substantial protective effect that operates independently of the other clinical parameters.

### 7.3 The Energy Budget and Bipolar Oscillation

The inclusion of energy as a latent state enables bipolar cycling to emerge from precision dynamics without hard-coding mood states. The oscillation follows a natural cycle: (1) FUTURATE depletes energy; (2) depletion degrades environmental outcomes; (3) negative outcomes eventually overwhelm blurred interoceptive beliefs; (4) the agent transitions to REST; (5) energy recovers; (6) FUTURATE becomes attractive again.

This switching dynamical system structure — driven by miscalibrated precision over a metabolic self-model constraint ($\omega_e$) — resonates with clinical accounts of bipolar disorder as a disorder of energy regulation (Berk et al., 2007). The model predicts that interventions targeting interoceptive accuracy (e.g., interoceptive training, somatic therapies) would reduce oscillation amplitude independently of interventions targeting narrative capacity ($\pi_{\text{pos}}$) or emotional granularity ($K$).

### 7.4 Two Formulations of Valence

The dual affective readout — Pattisapu et al.'s reward prediction error ($V = U - \mathbb{E}[U]$) alongside Joffily & Coricelli's temporal derivative ($-\dot{F}$) — reveals complementary aspects of affective dynamics. The Pattisapu formulation captures the *momentary* surprise of an outcome relative to expectations, and when combined with posterior entropy as arousal, provides the two-dimensional circumplex representation needed to distinguish states (like anger vs. sadness) that share valence but differ in arousal. The Joffily-Coricelli formulation captures the *trajectory* — whether things are getting better or worse over time — and its second derivative yields anticipatory emotions (hope, fear) that are central to the temporal framing account.

In the healthy agent, these two signals are correlated but not redundant. In pathological regimes, they dissociate: the manic agent may show positive Joffily-Coricelli valence (VFE is decreasing as FUTURATE "works" locally) while Pattisapu valence oscillates (actual outcomes are unpredictably better or worse than expected). This dissociation — between the trajectory feeling "right" and the momentary outcomes being volatile — may correspond to the clinical observation that manic patients report subjective wellbeing even as their behaviour becomes increasingly dysregulated.

### 7.5 Limitations and Future Directions

The current model treats temporal framing as a single-step policy selection rather than a deep temporal hierarchy. In reality, narrative processes unfold across nested timescales — a point of contact with Hesp et al.'s (2021) deep temporal model of valence. Extending the model to a hierarchical POMDP, with the temporal frame itself being inferred at a higher level, would capture the dynamics of narrative entrenchment and flexibility more naturally.

The simulation uses a fixed environment. In clinical reality, agents' framing strategies alter their environments (e.g., manic overcommitment creates real-world consequences). Closing this loop — so that FUTURATE selection *actually* depletes environmental resources, not just internal energy — would strengthen the ecological validity of the model.

Finally, while the granularity sweep provides a clear computational prediction (higher $K$ → lower oscillation amplitude), this prediction awaits empirical testing. One approach would be to measure emotional granularity (via experience sampling) in bipolar patients across mood states and correlate it with mood instability metrics.
